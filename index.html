Block 2: What We Learned from Research

3 Strongest Patterns

1. Learning Behavior Varies by Educational Stage

Primary, intermediate, and secondary students have fundamentally different learning behaviors, attention patterns, and motivations. A one-size-fits-all UX will fail.

2. Video Attention Spans Differ by Stage

Younger students need shorter, more structured video segments. Older students tolerate and prefer longer, deeper content. Content duration must adapt to stage.

3. Parents Buy Outcomes, Not Engagement

Parents make the purchasing decision. They pay for grades, exam readiness, and measurable academic progress — not for gamification, streaks, or social features.

Presenter: Islam (UX Research)

Time: 3 minutes

  • Present exactly 3 patterns — no more. Each one should feel like a discovery, not a confirmation.
  • For each pattern, briefly state what we observed and what design change it caused.
  • Pattern 3 is the most important commercially — parents are the buyer, students are the user.

Do NOT: Present more than 3 patterns. Do not speculate beyond what interviews showed. Do not claim statistical significance.

Block 3: How Research Changed Product Decisions

Product Strategy: 3 Decisions

1. Offline-First Is Essential, Not Optional

This is not a "nice to have." Iraqi infrastructure makes this a hard requirement. Every feature must work offline or gracefully degrade. This drives our entire architecture.

2. Exam-Driven Content, Not Passive Consumption

Content is organized around what students need for exams. Every lesson connects to assessable outcomes. Practice questions, not just videos. Measurable progress, not watch time.

3. Stage-Based Experience

Primary students get structured, guided paths. Intermediate students get more autonomy. Secondary students get exam-focused intensive tools. One product, three experiences.

POC 1

Offline Sync Validation

Hypothesis

Students can complete learning activities offline and sync reliably when connectivity returns.

Why This Matters

Iraq has unreliable internet. Learning cannot depend on constant connectivity. This is the foundation of everything else.

What We Tested & Result

  • PowerSync integration on iOS/Swift
  • 17 tables synced with full offline CRUD

  • 8 integration tests proving data integrity, hierarchy integrity, and write-path uploads

Limitations & Production Implications

  • Android upload path still needs implementation
  • Architecture proven for read-heavy offline use
  • Write conflicts need policy decisions for exam submissions

Block 4: Technical De-Risking
POC 4

Exam Integrity

Hypothesis

Server-side time validation and session monitoring can prevent common cheating vectors.

What We Tested

  • Server-authoritative timing (client cannot manipulate exam duration)
  • Session tracking (exam attempt lifecycle)
  • Schema supports exam timing and attempt tracking

What We Can Detect

  • Time manipulation (server is source of truth)
  • Multiple submission attempts
  • Session duration anomalies

What We Cannot Detect (Yet)

  • Tab switching / app backgrounding on mobile
  • Screen sharing or external cameras
  • In-person collaboration
  • These require policy mitigation, not technical prevention

Presenter: Robera

Time: 2 minutes

  • Frame honestly: "We can detect X. We cannot detect Y. Y requires policy mitigation, not technical prevention."
  • This is an architecture decision, not a complete anti-cheating solution.
  • The key message: server is the source of truth for timing. Students cannot manipulate exam duration on the client.
  • Policy decisions needed from client: What happens when cheating is detected? What happens when it is suspected but not confirmed?

Do NOT: Claim we have a complete anti-cheating system. Be explicit about boundaries. This builds trust.
